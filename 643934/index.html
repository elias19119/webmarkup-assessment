Haarlem centre

The Grote Kerk

The construction of saint bavokerk was first mentioned in 1245. However, the actual mention of a church in this spot in Haarlem was in 1307. The church has a unique architecture thanks to the architects who used the “Gothic” style of architecture. Unfortunately, in the 14th century the wooden structure was burned so the church was rebuilt and promoted to a chapter church in 1479. In 1559 The building went through a renovation to make it bigger than its original size until it was declared as a cathedral, this also took place during the Protestant reformation. the church was confiscated only 19 years later during the Haarlemse noon in 1578, when it was converted to Protestantism. Sadly, in on the 22 of May 1801 the tower of the church caught fire due to a lightning strike. Unexpectedly, another disaster was prevented in 1839 by Martijn Hendrik Kretschman, the guard of the tower. When he stopped Jan Drost a worker at church from setting fire to the pipe organ and piano when he attempted to throw hot coals on top of it. Drost committed suicide and he was buried in the tower.

The exterior of the church style has changed twice only in the past 500 years; once when all statuary was removed from the outer niches during the Haarlemse Noon in 1578, and the second time in the late 19th century when an idea of giving the church a "more Gothic look" was discussed. the idea was adding some fake ramparts to the roof edge. This can be easily noticed when comparing pictures made before and afterward the change. the church is surrounded by a low Hight buildings and the former fish market called De Vishal, which today is used for exhibiting modern art. In 1630 the architect Salomon de Bray designed and built the consistory which still exists today.
